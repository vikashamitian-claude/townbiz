# BizTown — Entrepreneur Framework (v2)
*(formerly "Knowledge Map" — file kept as `KNOWLEDGE_MAP.md` to preserve references)*

The complete set of things a player learns, from first shop to empire — on **two fronts at once**:
the **business** (skills) and **the person behind it** (entrepreneurial mindset). A university
syllabus **disguised as a game** — the structure that makes BizTown teach without ever feeling
like a course.

This is **Step 3** of the roadmap (Vision → Prototype Mission → **Framework** → Mission Tree
→ Economy → Code). It defines *what* is taught and *in what order*, so pacing, economy, and code
can follow.

> Most business games teach *how business works.* BizTown also teaches *how entrepreneurs think.*
> After 200 hours the player shouldn't just say "I know more about business" — they should say
> **"I think differently now."** That is the differentiator, and it's woven in from the start.

---

## How to read this map

Each concept is written as a **felt problem**, not a topic — because that's how it's taught:

> **Concept** — *discipline* — **Trigger problem** (what the player hits) → **Principle** (the one
> lesson, learned through consequence, never lectured).

**Two structural rules govern the whole map:**

- **Need-driven unlocking** — a concept appears only when the player's growing business *forces*
  the problem. Never taught before it's needed.
- **Spiral curriculum** — core concepts (pricing, people, cash, risk) **return at each phase at a
  higher altitude.** Same spine, deeper surface. This is deliberate.

Disciplines used: **Strategy · Finance · Inventory · Operations · Marketing · HR · Logistics · Tech ·
Leadership.**

---

## The two axes of growth

BizTown grows the player on **two axes at once** — and most business games move only the first:

- **Vertical — what you build (scale):** Tiny Shop → Store → Warehouse → Factory → Empire.
- **Vertical — who you become (identity):** Dreamer → Shopkeeper → Business Owner → Employer →
  Industrialist → Business Leader → Empire Builder. An earned, evolving **title**.
- **Horizontal — how you think (style):** your mindset *fingerprint* — bold or cautious, trusting
  or self-reliant, premium or value, honest or corner-cutting. **There is no single "correct"
  entrepreneur.** Many styles build empires, each with its own culture and risks. The game reveals
  *your* style; it never prescribes one.

The player should feel that **they** are evolving, not just their buildings.

## Design Law — Dual Growth

> **Every mission teaches one business concept AND strengthens one entrepreneurial mindset.**

With one non-negotiable refinement, or it becomes a motivational poster:

> **Mindset is earned through the *decision*, never *labeled* on it.** We never show
> "Mindset: Courage +1." A mission teaches courage because its choice *requires a brave bet and the
> consequence honors it* — not because we printed the word. The mindset lives in the **risk/reward
> of the choice**, not in a sticker the player reads. (The soft-skill version of "felt, never lectured.")

Example — the mindset is *in the mechanic*:

| Mission | Business concept | Mindset — *and how the choice embodies it* |
|---|---|---|
| Hire Ravi | Delegation | **Trust** — you hand a stranger your till; trusting earns loyalty, controlling stalls growth. |
| Competitor opens | Competition | **Adaptability** — the rigid price-cut bleeds you; the creative differentiation wins. |
| Expand the shop | Investment | **Courage** — a big, hard-to-reverse bet with real downside; safety means slower growth. |
| Start manufacturing | Vertical integration | **Vision** — short-term cost for long-term margin; impatience leaves money on the table. |

## Mindset threads (spiral — never phase-locked)

Like the business disciplines, mindsets recur and deepen across phases (correcting any phase-by-phase
reading — Trust starts at Ravi in P2, not P4):

| Mindset | How it's faced & deepens |
|---|---|
| **Vision** | dream big (P1) → spot opportunity (P3) → reinvent (P5) → legacy & contribution |
| **Courage & initiative** | take the leap (P1) → expand (P3) → invest big / acquire (P4–5) |
| **Resilience** | don't fear failure — recover from every setback, all phases (never game-over) |
| **Responsibility & discipline** | show up daily, cover the month, keep promises (P2+) |
| **Trust & leadership** | first hire (P2) → develop people (P3) → managers & culture (P4–5) |
| **Adaptability & creativity** | solve problems under change — competition, complaints (P3+) |
| **Integrity & value creation** | honest dealing → CSR → values under pressure → nation-building |

## How mindset is surfaced (no new system needed)

The **trait tags** already in the prototype spec (`pricing`, `risk`, `people`, `integrity`) are the
*data layer* of mindset. Options are tagged; tallies form the player's fingerprint. It surfaces only:

1. **The world reacts** — a team led by trust behaves differently than one led by pressure.
2. **The mirror** — at chapter ends, the game names who you've become from *your own* choices
   (*"You build by trust and bold bets — a founder's instinct"*), never as a grade.
3. **The evolving title** — the identity ladder above.

> **Trait tags = data · Mindset threads = meaning · Identity ladder = the felt title.**
> One system, three views. The player is never told — only shown.

## Phase 1 — Starting a Business
*Survival question: "Can I open and make my first sale?"*

| Concept | Discipline | Trigger problem → Principle |
|---|---|---|
| Choosing a business | Strategy | "What do you build?" → Match the opportunity to your means and interest. |
| Location | Strategy/Marketing | "Cheap corner or busy street?" → Footfall vs. cost is your first trade-off. |
| Startup investment | Finance | "₹10,000 — spend it on what?" → Allocate limited capital before you earn any. |
| Pricing (basic margin) | Finance | "What's your price?" → Profit lives between too-high and too-low. |
| Opening stock | Inventory | "How much to buy?" → Don't over-buy (cash frozen) or under-buy (lost sales). |
| The first customer | Marketing | "Nobody knows you exist." → The first sale must be *earned*, not assumed. |

## Phase 2 — Running a Business
*Stability question: "Can I keep it alive day to day?"*

| Concept | Discipline | Trigger problem → Principle |
|---|---|---|
| Customer service | Operations/Marketing | "A customer complains." → Service is how trust (reputation) is built. |
| Supplier relationships | Logistics | "Supplier is late / hikes price." → Reliability and negotiation matter as much as cost. |
| Cash flow | Finance | "Rent + wages due, but cash is tied up." → Profit on paper isn't money in hand. |
| Working capital | Finance | "Can't restock — money's stuck in stock." → Money must keep moving to keep you alive. |
| First employee (delegation) | HR/Leadership | "You can't cope alone." → You grow only as far as your own two hands — until you trust someone's. |
| Daily operations & consistency | Operations | "Quality slips when you're busy." → Systems keep quality steady when you can't. |

## Phase 3 — Growing
*Momentum question: "Can I get ahead and stand out?"*

| Concept | Discipline | Trigger problem → Principle |
|---|---|---|
| Competition | Strategy | "A rival opens nearby." → Compete on value, not just price. |
| Quality & reputation | Operations | "Complaints rise as you scale." → Quality compounds into reputation. |
| Pricing II — value & premium | Finance/Marketing | "Can I charge more now?" → Reputation earns pricing power; premium must be justified. |
| Branding & identity | Marketing | "Customers prefer branded goods." → A brand earns loyalty and a price premium. |
| Marketing & promotion | Marketing | "Sales plateau." → Reach drives demand; spend to grow. |
| Process improvement | Operations | "Bottlenecks slow everything." → Improve the system, not just your effort. |
| Employee development | HR/Leadership | "Ravi keeps making mistakes." → Train and grow people instead of blaming them. |
| Expansion (2nd location) | Strategy/Finance | "One shop can't meet demand." → Know *when* and *how* to expand. |

## Phase 4 — Scaling
*Leverage question: "Can the business grow beyond me?"*

| Concept | Discipline | Trigger problem → Principle |
|---|---|---|
| Warehousing | Logistics | "Stock overflows the shop." → Storage and bulk economics unlock scale. |
| Manufacturing | Operations/Strategy | "Demand's big enough to make your own." → Vertical integration captures more margin. |
| Distribution | Logistics | "Supply many shops on time." → Moving goods reliably at scale is its own discipline. |
| Multiple branches | Strategy/HR | "You can't run them all yourself." → Managers + standards replace your hands. |
| Technology & systems | Tech/Operations | "Manual tracking breaks down." → Tools multiply your reach. |
| Financing growth | Finance | "Growth needs capital you don't have." → Debt and investment are tools — with a cost. |

## Phase 5 — Empire
*Legacy question: "What outlasts me?"*

| Concept | Discipline | Trigger problem → Principle |
|---|---|---|
| Diversification | Strategy | "One market is fragile." → Multiple businesses spread risk. |
| Acquisitions & partnerships | Strategy/Finance | "Buy the rival? Partner up?" → Grow by buying, not only building. |
| Innovation | Strategy | "Your old model is fading." → Reinvent before the market forces you to. |
| Leadership at scale | Leadership | "Your people now lead others." → Your style becomes the company's culture. |
| Corporate culture | Leadership | "Values tested under pressure." → Culture is whatever you rewarded all along. |
| Succession & legacy | Leadership/Strategy | "Who carries it forward?" → The highest skill is letting go of what you built. |

## Cross-cutting threads (run through *every* phase, deepening)

These are **not** chapters — they spiral upward across the whole game:

| Thread | P1 | P2 | P3 | P4 | P5 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Leadership & people** | — | first hire | develop them | managers | culture & succession |
| **Financial literacy** | margin | cash flow / working capital | value pricing | financing | capital allocation |
| **Customers & trust** | first sale | service | brand & loyalty | multi-branch consistency | market reputation |
| **Integrity & CSR** | honest dealing | month-end choices | CSR → brand | standards at scale | values under pressure |
| **Risk & resilience** | stock/cash bets | survive the month | expansion risk | financing risk | diversification |

*Resilience note:* setbacks recur in every phase and are **never game-over** — recovering *is* the
lesson (see the prototype's no-hard-game-over guardrail).

## Coverage check (every discipline is taught, and re-taught)

- **Finance:** P1 margin → P2 cash flow/working capital → P3 value pricing → P4 financing → P5 allocation
- **Inventory:** P1 opening stock → P2 reordering → P4 warehousing
- **HR/Leadership:** P2 first hire → P3 development → P4 managers → P5 culture & succession
- **Operations:** P2 consistency → P3 quality & process → P4 manufacturing
- **Marketing:** P1 first sale → P3 branding & promotion
- **Logistics:** P2 suppliers → P4 warehousing & distribution
- **Strategy:** P1 choosing/location → P3 competition/expansion → P5 diversification/acquisitions
- **Tech:** P4 systems

## What this enables next (Step 4 — Mission Tree)

Every concept above becomes one or more **missions**, grouped into **chapters** that map to the five
phases. The prototype's "Save My First Shop" already covers most of Phase 1 + the front of Phase 2.

Mapping rule for the Mission Tree (the **Dual Growth Law**): **each mission teaches exactly one
business concept AND strengthens one entrepreneurial mindset** — the mindset earned through the
choice, never labeled. It unlocks only when its trigger problem can plausibly arise, advances at
least one cross-cutting thread, and moves the player along the identity ladder. Nothing random —
every mission grows both the business *and* the entrepreneur.
